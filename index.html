SECTION 1 = FACTS ABOUT THE ORTHODOX CHURCH

*The Orthodox Churech*, also known as the Eastern Orthodox Church, is one of the oldest branches of Christianity, tracing its roots directly back to the apostles and the early Christian communities established in the Eastern part of the Roman Empire. It formally began to take shape as distinct from the Western (Roman Catholic) Church following centuries of cultural, political, and theological differences, which eventually led to the Great Schism of 1054. This split occurred when mutual excommunications between the Patriarch of Constantinople and the Pope of Rome divided Christianity into the Roman Catholic Church in the West and the Orthodox Church in the East. The Orthodox Church places great emphasis on preserving the original teachings and traditions of the early Church, including the use of liturgy, icons, fasting, and sacraments as central parts of worship. It also upholds the principle of apostolic succession, meaning that its bishops are seen as the direct spiritual descendants of the apostles, maintaining an unbroken line of authority and faith from the time of Christ.

*Apostolic succession* is the authority and spiritual leadership that has been handed down from Jesus Christ to His apostles and, through them, to the bishops of the Church throughout history. This unbroken line of succession means that today’s Orthodox bishops are seen as the direct successors of the original apostles. Through this connection, bishops (and the priests they ordain) share in the apostolic authority to teach, lead, and administer the holy mysteries (sacraments) of the Church, such as the Eucharist and the forgiveness of sins. Bishops are considered “married to the Church,” dedicating their lives fully to serving God and guiding the faithful.

*THE GREAT SCHISM IN 1054* = The Great Schism was the official split between the Eastern Orthodox Church and the Roman Catholic Church, marking one of the most significant divisions in Christian history. Although tensions had been building for centuries due to cultural, political, and theological differences, the final break came in 1054. One major disagreement was over the Filioque clause, an addition by the Western Church to the Nicene Creed stating that the Holy Spirit “proceeds from the Father and the Son,” which the Eastern Church rejected. Other disputes included the authority of the Pope, differences in worship practices, and misunderstandings between the Greek-speaking East and the Latin-speaking West. In 1054, mutual excommunications between the Pope’s representatives and the Patriarch of Constantinople made the division official. Despite this separation, both Churches share the same early Christian roots and many core beliefs that date back to the time of the apostles.

SECTION 2 =ORTHODOX WORSHIP TRADITIONS

Orthodox worship is deeply spiritual and rich in symbolism, focusing on bringing believers into the presence of God. The central act of worship is the Divine Liturgy, where the faithful gather to receive the Body and Blood of Christ in the Eucharist. Every part of the service is filled with meaning — the prayers, hymns, and readings are all expressions of praise and thanksgiving to God. The Orthodox Church uses incense, candles, and icons to create a holy atmosphere that engages all the senses and reminds the faithful that they are standing before heaven itself.

The Divine Liturgy = The Divine Liturgy is the heart of Orthodox worship, celebrated every Sunday and on feast days. It has remained mostly unchanged for centuries, connecting believers today to the earliest Christians. During the Liturgy, the priest and deacons lead the congregation in prayers, hymns, and the Eucharist. The faithful stand throughout the service as a sign of respect and participation, joining together in worship as one body in Christ.

Use of Icons and Incense = Icons — sacred images of Christ, the Virgin Mary, and the saints — play an important role in Orthodox worship. They are not just decorations but “windows to heaven,” helping the faithful focus their hearts on God. Incense is also used during services to symbolize prayers rising to heaven and to honor the presence of the Holy Spirit. Together, icons and incense remind worshippers of the divine reality that surrounds them.

Chanting and Participation = Orthodox worship is mostly sung or chanted rather than spoken. The choir and congregation respond to the priest’s prayers through beautiful ancient melodies, creating a sense of harmony and unity. Worship in the Orthodox Church is not something to watch — it’s something to join in, heart and soul.

SECTION 3 = WHO IS THE THEOTOKOS?

The Theotokos, meaning "God-bearer" or "Mother of God," is a title given to the Virgin Mary in the Orthodox Church. This title emphasizes her role as the mother of Jesus Christ, who is both fully God and fully human. The Theotokos is venerated in Orthodox Christianity as the highest of all saints and the most important intercessor before God. The Orthodox Church teaches that Mary was chosen by God to bear His Son and that she remained a virgin before, during, and after the birth of Christ. Her life is seen as a model of faith, obedience, and humility for all Christians.

In the early centuries of Christianity, some groups denied the title of Theotokos, which means “God-bearer” or “Mother of God,” for the Virgin Mary. The most well-known of these was the Nestorian heresy, taught by Nestorius, the Patriarch of Constantinople in the 5th century. He argued that Mary should be called only the “Mother of Christ” (Christotokos), not the “Mother of God,” because he believed that Jesus’ divine and human natures were separate. The Orthodox Church rejected this teaching at the Council of Ephesus in 431 AD, declaring that Jesus Christ is one Person with both a divine and human nature united perfectly. Therefore, Mary truly gave birth to the Son of God incarnate and rightly bears the title Theotokos. Denying this title was seen as denying the true divinity of Christ Himself, which is why the Church firmly defended Mary’s role and honor.

SECTION 4 = THE HOLY TRINITY

The Holy Trinity is a central mystery of the Christian faith, teaching that there is one God in three persons: the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit. Each person is fully God, co-eternal and co-equal, yet there is only one divine essence. This mystery is not fully comprehensible to human reason but is accepted by faith. The Orthodox Church emphasizes the relational aspect of the Trinity, highlighting the love and communion shared among the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit.

SECTION 5 = THE SACRAMENTS

In this section you will learn about the 7 sacraments.

What are the sacraments or better known as "mysteries"? (ALL THE VERSES LISTED IN THIS WEBSITE ARE IN NIV)

The sacraments are visible signs of God's grace, instituted by Christ, to give us divine life. They are:

  • Baptism
  • Eucharist
  • Confirmation
  • Reconciliation
  • Anointing of the Sick
  • Holy Orders
  • Matrimony

Baptism

Baptism is the first sacrament of initiation. It is a rite of passage that signifies the individual's entry into the Christian community. Through baptism, one is cleansed of original sin and reborn as a child of God. It is often performed with water, symbolizing purification and the new life in Christ. John 3:5 "Very truly I tell you, no one can enter the kingdom of God unless they are born of water and the Spirit"

Eucharist

The Eucharist, also known as Holy Communion, is the second sacrament of initiation. It is the sacrament in which the body and blood of Christ are truly present under the appearances of bread and wine. The Eucharist is the source and summit of Christian life, nourishing the faithful and uniting them with Christ. John 6:53 is a verse from the Bible where Jesus states, "Truly, truly, I say to you, unless you eat the flesh of the Son of Man and drink his blood, you do not have life within you".

Confirmation

Confirmation is the third sacrament of initiation. It is the sacrament in which the Holy Spirit strengthens the baptized person, enabling them to live out their faith more fully. Confirmation is often conferred by a bishop and involves the anointing with chrism oil. Acts 8:14 to 17 “When the apostles in Jerusalem heard that Samaria had accepted the word of God, they sent Peter and John to Samaria. When they arrived, they prayed for the new believers there that they might receive the Holy Spirit… Then Peter and John placed their hands on them, and they received the Holy Spirit.”

Reconciliation

Reconciliation, also known as Confession, is the sacrament of healing. It is the means by which sins committed after baptism are forgiven. Through the sacrament of Reconciliation, the faithful experience God's mercy and are reconciled with the Church. James 5:16 “Therefore confess your sins to each other and pray for each other so that you may be healed. The prayer of a righteous person is powerful and effective.”

Anointing of the Sick

The Anointing of the Sick is a sacrament of healing administered to those who are seriously ill or near death. Through the anointing with oil and the prayers of the Church, the sick person receives spiritual strength, peace, and, if God wills, physical healing. James 5:14 to 15 “Is anyone among you sick? Let them call the elders of the church to pray over them and anoint them with oil in the name of the Lord. And the prayer offered in faith will make the sick person well; the Lord will raise them up. If they have sinned, they will be forgiven.”

Holy Orders

Holy Orders is the sacrament through which men are ordained as bishops, priests, or deacons. It confers a special grace and empowers the ordained to serve the Church and administer the sacraments. John 20:21 to 23“Again Jesus said, ‘Peace be with you! As the Father has sent me, I am sending you.’ And with that he breathed on them and said, ‘Receive the Holy Spirit. If you forgive anyone’s sins, their sins are forgiven; if you do not forgive them, they are not forgiven.’”

Matrimony

Matrimony, also known as marriage, is the sacrament that unites a man and a woman in a lifelong covenant of love and fidelity. It signifies the mutual commitment to support each other and to raise a family in accordance with God's will. The sacrament of Matrimony is a reflection of Christ's love for the Church. Mark 10:7–9 "‘For this reason a man will leave his father and mother and be united to his wife, and the two will become one flesh.’ So they are no longer two, but one flesh. Therefore what God has joined together, let no one separate.”
